# boxscore — Ad Sales Playbook

A starter guide for selling advertising in the boxscore daily email. Written for
someone brand new to this. You do **not** need to be technical. You need to
understand the product, be honest about where it is, and have a real conversation
with a local business owner.

Read this once top to bottom, then keep it open during your first few calls.

---

## 1. What boxscore is (in plain English)

boxscore is a **daily email** that goes out every morning during the season. It's
the sports box scores — standings, league leaders, and the full line-by-line
results of yesterday's games — delivered to your inbox instead of you hunting for
them in a newspaper or app.

The whole point is that it looks and reads like the **sports page of an old
newspaper**: dense, clean, text-only, no flashing junk, no autoplay video, no
clickbait. People who subscribe are people who genuinely love the game and want
the numbers with their coffee.

A few things that matter for selling:

- It's **free** for readers. Always will be.
- It goes out **every day during the season** — that's a daily touchpoint, not a
  once-a-month thing.
- The audience is **self-selected sports fans** who opted in and open it on
  purpose. Nobody is here by accident.
- The look is **classy and quiet**. That's the brand. Our ads look like
  tasteful classified or sponsor lines in a real newspaper — never banner-ad
  garbage. (More on formats below.)

> One-line version you can say out loud: *"boxscore is a daily email of baseball
> box scores and standings, made to feel like the old newspaper sports page,
> read by people who really love the game."*

## 2. Be honest about where we are: this is the ground floor

**We have not sold a single ad yet. The ad program is brand new.** Do not pretend
otherwise. This is not a weakness — it's your best angle, if you're honest about
it:

- The businesses you sign now are **founding advertisers.** Ground-floor pricing,
  best slots, and a say in how the ads look.
- You're offering a **pilot / trial**, not a long contract. Low risk for them.
- We are building this *with* our first advertisers, not selling them a finished
  machine.

What this means for you: you are not closing big deals on day one. You are having
friendly conversations, finding 3–5 local businesses willing to try it, and
learning what questions come up. That's success right now.

**Never make up numbers, never promise results we can't prove.** If you don't know
something, say "great question, let me find out and get back to you." That builds
more trust than a confident guess. (This honesty is literally the brand —
boxscore is data, not hype.)

## 3. Who to talk to first

Local, sports-adjacent, owner-operated businesses are the natural fit — the same
kinds of businesses that advertised in newspaper sports sections for a hundred
years:

- Sporting goods stores, batting cages, card & memorabilia shops
- Bars and restaurants near a ballpark or that show every game
- Local radio stations, ticket resellers, custom uniform / screen-print shops
- Anything owner-run where you can get the decision-maker in one conversation

Skip national chains for now — they buy ads through agencies and won't take a
cold call from a new program. Stay local, stay personal.

## 4. The ad formats we offer (what you're actually selling)

There are **four** formats, listed cheapest/least-intrusive to most premium. You
do not need to memorize the HTML — just know what each one *is* and where it sits
in the email.

| Format | What it looks like | Where it sits | Sold as |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Classified** | A few lines of typed text under a "CLASSIFIEDS" header, like a newspaper classified | Near the bottom | Cheapest, sold in bulk to local shops |
| **Standings strip** | A thin one-line band with the business name + tagline | Between sections | One advertiser per strip |
| **Display box** | A bordered box with a headline and a couple lines, like a small newspaper display ad | Between major sections | More premium, more visual weight |
| **Sponsor line** | One elegant italic line: *"Today's edition brought to you by ___"* | Very top, under the date | **Most premium** — only one per day, no competition |

Real examples we've mocked up (so you can picture it):

- *Sponsor line:* "— Today's edition brought to you by **Henderson Sporting
  Goods**, outfitting weekend ballplayers since 1962 —"
- *Classified:* "**BATTING CAGES —** Northgate, open 7 days. Bring this newsletter,
  $5 off any hour."
- *Display box:* a bordered "**Greenfield Lawn & Garden** — Spring opening
  weekend…" box.

Every ad is **clickable** and sends the reader to the advertiser's website. And
because the whole email looks like a newspaper, the ad fits right in instead of
fighting the content.

> If a business asks "what will my ad look like?" — we can show them a live
> mockup of their own ad slotted into a real edition before they pay anything.
> (Ask Jeff to generate one for the specific business; that preview is one of our
> strongest closing tools.)

## 5. The audience numbers

*Pulled from the live system on **June 16, 2026**. These move daily — ask Jeff to
refresh them every few weeks (he can re-run `scripts/diag-ad-sales-numbers.ts`).
Always quote the current figure, not a stale one.*

**The headline numbers you can say out loud:**

- **About 6,000 daily readers** (6,091 active subscribers) — every one of them
  asked to receive it.
- **Growing fast: roughly 2,000+ new readers a month.** In the last 30 days we
  added ~2,700 new subscribers and lost ~400, for net growth of ~2,300. That's a
  list getting bigger every week, not a stale one.
- **It gets opened — a lot.** On the conservative measure, **about 1 in 3 emails
  we send is opened every day (~35%)**, and by stricter industry math (opens vs.
  confirmed deliveries) it's higher. For email, that's a strong number — most
  marketing email runs 15–25%. These people open it *daily*, not once a month.

**Your simple, safe pitch line:**
> "About 6,000 people get boxscore every single morning, we're adding a couple
> thousand more a month, and roughly a third or more open it every day. They
> asked for it — these are real fans, not a bought list."

**Be honest about two things (this is the brand — never fudge it):**

1. **Open rates everywhere are inflated by Apple's Mail Privacy feature**, which
   auto-loads the tracking pixel whether or not a human read it. So treat open
   numbers as an *upper bound*. If an advertiser pushes on it, say exactly that —
   it builds trust, and it's true of every newsletter they'll ever buy.
2. **Click rate is low (well under 1%)** — and that's *expected*, not a problem.
   People read the box scores right inside the email; they're not here to click
   away. So sell boxscore as a **daily-visibility / brand play in front of a loyal
   audience**, not a click-machine. (If an advertiser only cares about clicks,
   they're a worse fit than one who wants to be seen by 6,000 local fans every
   morning.)

**What we do NOT know yet — don't claim it:**

- **Reader location.** We don't track where subscribers live, so don't promise a
  specific city or region. It's US baseball, so it's US baseball fans — but don't
  invent a "mostly [city]" number. If an advertiser needs local targeting, flag
  it to Jeff; everyone currently gets the same edition.

## 6. Questions advertisers will ask — and how to answer

Memorize the shape of these. You don't need the perfect words; you need to not be
caught off guard.

**"How many people will see my ad?"**
> "About 6,000 people get it every morning, and roughly a third or more open it
> every single day. They opted in and read it on purpose." *(Use the current
> numbers from Section 5.)*

**"Who reads it? What's the audience?"**
> "Sports fans who love the box scores — people who'd have read the newspaper
> sports page. They're engaged; nobody's here by accident." *(Don't claim a
> specific city — we don't track location. See Section 5.)*

**"How much does it cost?"**
> "Since you'd be one of our founding advertisers, we're keeping it simple and
> affordable to start — [pricing TBD with Jeff]. I'd rather get you in at a
> ground-floor rate and prove it works than overcharge you on day one."
> *(Get the real price/packages from Jeff before quoting anything.)*

**"What will my ad actually look like?"**
> "It looks like a tasteful newspaper ad — clean text, fits the page, no flashing
> banners. I can show you a mockup of *your* business in a real edition before you
> commit a dollar."

**"How do I know it works? Can I see results / clicks?"**
> "Every ad links to your site, and the plan is for advertisers to get their own
> dashboard with views and clicks. Right now we're early, so I'll be honest about
> what we can and can't measure yet — but a daily email to engaged fans is a real
> touchpoint." *(Don't promise tracking we don't have live yet — be straight.)*

**"Can I target just my city / just my sport?"**
> "Today everyone gets the same edition, so your ad reaches the whole list. More
> targeting is on the roadmap." *(True today — don't oversell targeting.)*

**"How long do I have to commit?"**
> "No long contract. Let's start with a short run — a week or a month — and see
> how it feels."

**"Why should I do this instead of Facebook/Instagram ads?"**
> "On social you're one of fifty things flying by. Here you're in a calm daily
> email that people actually open and read, next to content they came for — and
> you're supporting something local. It's the newspaper sports page, not a feed."

**"Is this just baseball? What about other sports?"**
> "It's built around baseball today, and built to grow into other sports."
> *(Check with Jeff for the current status before promising any specific sport.)*

**"Can I change my ad / run a seasonal promotion?"**
> "Absolutely — that's the kind of thing we'll work out together. Bring me what
> you want to promote." *(Loop in Jeff on logistics.)*

**Any question you don't know:**
> "That's a great question — I don't want to guess, so let me get you the exact
> answer." Then ask Jeff. This is always the right move.

## 7. A basic script for a first conversation

This is a *guide*, not a word-for-word read. Sound like yourself. The goal of the
first conversation is **not to close** — it's to spark interest and earn a second
conversation or a mockup.

**Opening (warm, local, low-pressure):**
> "Hi, I'm [name]. I help with a daily email called boxscore — it's the baseball
> box scores and standings, made to feel like the old newspaper sports page, and
> it goes out every morning to fans around [area]. Do you have two minutes?"

**The hook (why them):**
> "We're just starting to offer advertising, and I thought of [business] because
> you're exactly the kind of local spot that used to live in the newspaper sports
> section. I wanted to see if you'd be open to being one of our first
> advertisers."

**What it is (keep it short):**
> "It's a clean, text-style ad — looks like a classy newspaper sponsor line or
> classified, not a flashy banner. It links right to your site. Every reader
> opted in, so these are real fans, not random clicks."

**The low-risk offer:**
> "Because you'd be a founding advertiser, the pricing is ground-floor and there's
> no long commitment — we can start with a short run and see how it feels. And I
> can show you exactly what your ad would look like in a real edition before you
> decide anything."

**The ask (small, concrete next step):**
> "Could I put together a quick mockup of [business] in tomorrow's style and send
> it over? If you like it, we go from there. No pressure either way."

**If they say yes:** Get their **email**, their **website**, and one line about
**what they'd want to promote** (a sale, their hours, a discount). Bring that to
Jeff for a mockup.

**If they're hesitant:**
> "Totally fair. Can I email you a one-pager and a sample so you can look on your
> own time?" Get the email. A 'maybe later' with contact info is a win.

**Always close friendly:**
> "Either way, thanks for the time — and the box scores are free, so subscribe
> yourself if you're a fan." (Getting an advertiser to read the product helps a
> lot.)

## 8. Your first-week goals (so success is clear)

You are new and this program is new. "Success" right now is small and concrete:

1. Be able to explain boxscore in two sentences without notes.
2. Get the real audience numbers and pricing from Jeff and write them into
   Section 5.
3. Have 5 friendly conversations with local businesses — calls or walk-ins.
4. Collect 3 emails of businesses willing to see a mockup.
5. Bring Jeff a list of the questions you got that you *couldn't* answer — those
   make this playbook better.

That's it. No big closes required. You're learning the product and planting
seeds.

---

## 9. The golden rules

- **Never invent a number or promise a result.** "Let me find out" always beats a
  guess. This is the whole brand — we sell data, not hype.
- **Stay local and personal.** This program wins one owner-to-owner conversation
  at a time.
- **Lead with the mockup.** Showing a business their own ad in a real edition is
  worth more than any pitch.
- **Honesty about being early is a feature**, not something to hide. Ground floor,
  low risk, founding advertiser.
- **When in doubt, ask Jeff.** Better a slightly slower answer than a wrong one.
